## **── SECTION: THE PRINCIPLES ──**

*\[Design note: 4 short, clean principle statements in a 2×2 grid. Each has a bold one-line principle and 2 sentences of context. This sets the philosophy before the mechanics.\]*

**Section eyebrow (small caps, orange):** THE WAY I OPERATE

---

**Principle 1:** **Diagnosis before prescription.** I won't tell you what to build until I understand what's broken. The symptom and the problem are usually different things — and fixing the symptom without addressing the root is expensive.

**Principle 2:** **Systems over campaigns.** A campaign ends. A system compounds. Every engagement is designed to leave you with something that keeps running after the project closes — not a one-time deliverable that expires.

**Principle 3:** **One accountable person.** I don't manage a team that does your work. I do your work. That means there's one place to look when something isn't delivering — and one person who will fix it.

**Principle 4:** **Honest over comfortable.** If what you're asking me to build won't solve the actual problem, I'll tell you before we start. This has cost me engagements. It's also why clients come back.

## **── SECTION: HOW ENGAGEMENTS WORK ──**

*\[Design note: Numbered vertical steps. Large step number in JetBrains Mono (orange), bold step name in Syne, body description in Inter. Clean, linear flow. This section demystifies "what happens if I reach out."\]*

**Section eyebrow (small caps, orange):** THE ENGAGEMENT SEQUENCE

**Section headline (H2):** Here's exactly what happens when you reach out.

---

**Step 01:** **You start the conversation.** A message through the contact form is enough. Tell me what's going on — as much or as little as you know. I read every message myself and reply within one business day, usually faster.

**Step 02:** **We do a 30-minute diagnostic session.** No deck. No pitch. I ask specific questions to understand the problem, the current state of your systems, and what's actually getting in the way. By the end, we both know whether this is the right fit.

**Step 03:** **I send a scoped proposal.** If we're a fit, I write a proposal based on what the diagnostic revealed — not a menu of services to choose from. The scope is specific, the timeline is honest, and the price is tied to outcomes.

**Step 04:** **We start with the foundation.** Every engagement starts with a structured baseline: what's working, what isn't, where the data actually points, and what the highest-leverage move is given the time and budget available. This isn't a formality — it's where half the real strategic insight comes from. Skipping it is how you end up building the wrong thing quickly.

**Step 05:** **I build it. You stay involved.** You're never handed a deliverable and left alone with it. I work in tight loops — sharing progress, explaining decisions, and adjusting based on what we learn as we go. The build process is transparent because the system has to make sense to you after I've set it up.

**Step 06:** **We measure it, not just launch it.** The work isn't done when the system goes live. We define success metrics before the build starts, instrument the reporting during the build, and evaluate performance together after launch. If something isn't producing, we fix it — not because the contract says so, but because the outcome is the point.

**Card 1 — Fractional / Ongoing** **Format name:** Fractional Engagement **One-liner:** Ongoing strategic ownership of your full marketing function. **Right for:** Companies that need a senior marketing lead but aren't ready to hire a full-time CMO or VP. You need someone who holds the whole system, not just executes a task list.

**What's included:**

* Weekly strategic check-ins and priority setting  
* Full oversight of marketing operations, systems, and team direction  
* CRM and automation monitoring and optimization  
* Reporting and attribution review  
* Direct availability for decision support and approvals

**Proof reference:** Graston Technique® — 18-month fractional engagement. Full MarTech ecosystem ownership. Result: \+212% qualified leads.

---

**Card 2 — Project-Based** **Format name:** Project Engagement **One-liner:** A defined scope, a clear deliverable, a fixed timeline. **Right for:** Companies with a specific problem to solve — a site rebuild, a CRM migration, an automation buildout, a positioning overhaul. The scope is clear, the outcome is defined, and the engagement has an end date.

**What's included:**

* Diagnostic and scoping session  
* Structured build or strategy deliverable  
* Implementation or execution (not just recommendations)  
* Handoff documentation and training  
* 30-day post-launch support window

**Proof reference:** Graston Growth Engine — custom automation platform build replacing 8 manual processes. Result: 95% less manual overhead.

---

**Card 3 — Advisory / Diagnostic** **Format name:** Advisory Session **One-liner:** A single structured session to diagnose the problem and map the path forward. **Right for:** Companies that aren't ready to commit to a full engagement but want expert eyes on their current situation. You leave with a specific diagnosis, a prioritized action plan, and the clarity to make the right next move — with or without me.

**What's included:**

* 90-minute structured diagnostic session  
* Pre-session data and materials review  
* Written summary with diagnosis, prioritized recommendations, and implementation notes  
* Optional follow-up call within 30 days

**Note:** Advisory sessions often become the starting point for ongoing or project engagements — but there's no obligation, and the session delivers real value on its own.

## **── SECTION: WHAT I DON'T DO ──**

*\[Design note: Simple list section. Dark background. Bold header. 4–5 short, direct lines. No explanations needed — the directness is the signal. This section builds trust by being honest about scope and fit.\]*

**Section eyebrow (small caps, orange):** HONEST ABOUT FIT

**Section headline (H2):** A few things I'm clear about upfront.

**List items (each one is bold first line \+ 1 sentence clarification):**

**I don't take on every client.** I keep my roster intentionally small so every client gets the full version of the work. If I'm at capacity or if it's not the right fit, I'll say so directly.

**I don't build spec work or free strategies.** The diagnostic session is structured and valuable — it's not a free consultation that turns into an unpaid strategy session. If you're not ready to engage, the free tools on this site will give you real direction.

**I don't guarantee specific outcomes before I've diagnosed the problem.** Any consultant who quotes you a result before understanding your situation is selling you a number, not a plan. I give you honest projections based on what I actually find.

**I don't do campaigns without systems.** If you need a single email campaign or a one-off social media push, I'm probably not the right fit. The work I do builds infrastructure — things that run and compound over time.

## **── SECTION: WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE ──**

*\[Design note: 3 scenario cards — each one describes a specific type of client situation and what the engagement produces. Written in second person ("you"). Dark surface cards, left-aligned. This section does the job of a FAQ without the FAQ format — it helps the right buyer self-identify before reaching out.\]*

**Section eyebrow (small caps, orange):** IS THIS RIGHT FOR YOU?

**Section headline (H2):** The work looks different depending on where you are.

---

**Scenario 1:** **You're a founder or executive who needs marketing to be someone else's problem.** You're running the business. You know marketing needs to be better. You don't have the bandwidth to own it yourself and you don't want to hire a full-time person yet. You need someone who will take the wheel, make good decisions, and tell you clearly what's working and why.

What good looks like: A fractional engagement where you check in weekly, you get clear reporting, and the marketing system actually runs without you in it.

---

**Scenario 2:** **You have a specific problem that needs to be fixed.** The CRM is a mess. The website doesn't convert. Attribution is a black box. You know what the problem is — you just need someone senior enough to scope it correctly and capable enough to actually build the fix.

What good looks like: A project engagement with a clear deliverable, a realistic timeline, and no hand-offs to junior people once the contract is signed.

---

**Scenario 3:** **You operate in a regulated or high-trust industry — marketing has to pass internal review, not just look good.** Healthcare, legal, finance — you're not just trying to generate leads. You're trying to maintain trust and credibility in an environment where one wrong claim creates real problems. You need someone who understands that tradeoff and respects it.

What good looks like: Execution that respects compliance culture — clear drafts, traceable claims, and patient collaboration with legal or clinical voices when required.
